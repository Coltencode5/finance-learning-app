### Preface (approx PDF page 11–12)
- **Argument:** Leslie Wexner (Limited Brands) endorses the book’s “sweet spot”: consumers will trade up when firms respect their taste and create demand. Limited shifted from promotions/real-estate dominance toward brands; Fortune named it “world’s most admired specialty retailer” in 2003. Victoria’s Secret is the book’s emblematic lingerie success.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Entrepreneur instinct + outside strategic partners (BCG); never assume the customer “won’t get it.”
- **Companies/episodes:** Limited’s first Columbus store (1963); Victoria’s Secret; Wexner’s European lingerie-boutique insight vs. U.S. department-store underwear habit; Starbucks parallel (Schultz/Italian coffee).

### Introduction to the Paperback Edition (approx PDF page 13–18)
- **Argument:** Written for 2008 paperback: trading up survived Iraq-war skepticism, housing-price shocks, and cycles; it is durable, global, and paired with trading down—“death in the middle.” New Luxury is a strategy: ladder of technical/functional/emotional benefits plus tight targeting, identity, innovation, cost-into-product, and apostles.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** New Luxury 15 Index; trade-up / trade-down / middle polarization; four emotional forces (take care of me, connect, quest, individual style); practices list (difference, tight targeting, identity/retail experience, innovation stream, invest cost for sell-side return, apostles).
- **Companies/episodes:** Coach as trading-up icon; China survey 2006–2007 (electronics, personal care, apparel/footwear, food/beverage).
- **Numbers committed:** 2003 estimate: 23 New Luxury categories ~$400B U.S., ~15%/yr toward ~$1T by decade end; New Luxury 15 avg sales growth 19% (2002–03) vs GDP ~3%; median TSR 26% (2001–03) vs S&P 500 ~4%. By 2006: ~10M households >$100k; trading-up ~21% of $3.5T spend ≈ $730B (from $670B prior year); trading-down $1.2T (2006) from $1.1T (2005)—33%/32%/31% of discretionary (2006/05/04). Middle share 51%→48%→46% (2004–06). Coach: ~$2B market value at first discussion → ~$2B revenue, 77% gross margin, ~$14B market value. Target consumers often $50k+. Teenagers cited with up to ~$600/month discretionary.
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 13–18
- **Gap:** Longitudinal figures mix hardcover-era forecasts with 2005–06 Treasure Hunt follow-ups; keep year tags.

### Chapter One — Trading Up to New Luxury: An Overview (approx PDF page 19–30)
- **Argument:** Middle-market consumers eagerly pay premiums for “New Luxury”—higher quality/taste/aspiration than conventional goods, still reachable. High price + high volume breaks the classic demand curve; categories polarize into trade-up, trade-down, and “death in the middle.”
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Three New Luxury types—accessible superpremium; Old Luxury brand extensions; masstige. Ladder of benefits (technical → functional → emotional). Rocketing; disharmony of consumption. Eight practices of New Luxury leaders (never underestimate customer; shatter price-volume curve; genuine benefit ladder; escalate innovation/quality/experience; extend brand price range; customize/orchestrate value chain; influence marketing & apostles; attack like outsider). Demand-side forces (wealth, women, family structure, education/travel/media) and supply-side (specialty retail, mass merchandisers stocking premium, globalization of supply chains).
- **Companies/episodes:** Jake (construction worker, Callaway clubs); Whirlpool Duet; Frances (jewelry after breakup); Belvedere vs Absolut; Nutro vs Alpo; Mercedes C-class & Maybach; Bath & Body Works vs Vaseline Intensive Care vs Kiehl’s; BMW wash/gaze behavior vs Chanel/Rolls status vs Taurus; Starbucks, Kendall-Jackson, Victoria’s Secret; Costco premium wine; Jim Koch/Boston Beer orchestration example.
- **Numbers committed:** Jake ~$50k income, ~$3k Callaway set vs <~$1k conventional; Callaway not top-10 in 1989 → #1 within 3 years of Big Bertha (1990). Survey >2,300 people $50k+; 96% pay premium in ≥1 category; ~48M U.S. households ≥$50k; avg HH size 2.6 ≈ ~125M people. Duet >$2k vs ~$600 conventional pair. 23 categories ~$2T total, New Luxury ~20% ≈ $400B, growing ~10–15%/yr; Europe similar; global New Luxury expected ~$2T by decade end. Belvedere ~$28 (~88% over Absolut ~$16); Nutro ~$0.71/lb (~58% over Alpo ~$0.45); Mercedes C-class coupe ~$26k; Maybach >$300k; BBW lotion $9/8oz vs Vaseline $3.29/11oz; Kiehl’s $24/8oz. Home equity pool ~$8T; avg homeowner equity ~$50k; ~$100B freed by discounters (2003 est.). Married-with-kids-at-home ~24% of households; ~half of marriages end in divorce; ~1/3 fail in first 10 years; ~1/4 married women earn more than husbands.
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 19–30

### Chapter Two — The Spenders and Their Needs (approx PDF page 31–64)
- **Argument:** No single demographic “typical” New Luxury buyer—behavior is selective rocketing. Rising real incomes, home equity, women’s earnings/hours, later marriage/fewer kids, education, travel, and media aspiration expand capacity and desire. Four emotional spaces organize why categories matter.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Selective trade-up/trade-down; four emotional spaces—Taking Care of Me; Connecting; Questing; Individual Style. Relative necessity; DINK households; brand narratives as learning.
- **Companies/episodes:** Kathy (Coach, wine, Panera); Nadyenka/Jake; Home Depot growth; American Girl Place outing; Canyon Ranch (Zuckermans); Aveda; Sony home theater vs Bose aspiration; spa day-spa democratization; Oprah as aspiration brand; adventure travel examples.
- **Numbers committed:** Survey: 96.2% pay more in ≥1 category; ~70% rocket in as many as 10; 48.4% spend “as much as they can” in a category. Real HH income +>50% (1970–2000); ~112M households; ~28M ≥$75k; 16M of those >$100k. Home equity ~$8T; avg unrealized gain ~$50k; nearly half took equity loan/LOC/2nd mortgage; 22% of refinancers took cash. Homes doubled in size since 1950; home spend $160B (2001) from $67B (1970); Home Depot $1.6B (1985) → $54B (2001). ≥$3T discretionary “created” over 30 years (authors’ estimate). Women 16–64 workforce 60%; 25–54: 76%; real median full-time women income $21,477 (1970) → $30,240 (2001) (+41%). Married-with-kids 24% of HH. Median first marriage age 20.8 (1970) → 25.1 (2000); median age first birth 22.5 → 26.5; first birth after 30: 18% → 38%. Birthrate 87.9 → 67.5 per 1,000; HH size 3.11 → 2.60; per-capita income $12,400 → ~$22,000 (+>75%). Europe travelers 3M (1970) → 11M (2000); Asia travel +93% (1990–2000). Restaurant visits/capita 153 (1991) → 178 (2001). Canyon Ranch: 350 guests, up to $6k/week, ~20% operating margin; spa industry U.S. revenues >$14B (2003); spa visits tripled since ~1997. Dating-to-marriage interval 1.3 → 8.1 years; divorce in first 10 years 20% (1973) → 33% (2000); lifetime divorce probability age 25 ≈ 52% (1996). ~40% survey respondents “worried about the future.” 70% say “knowledge is the greatest luxury.”
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 31–64
- **Gap:** Mid-chapter figure pages thin; emotional-space “Individual Style” section denser near end—confirm if architect needs more examples from pp. 59–64.

### Chapter Three — The Creators and Their Goods (approx PDF page 65–86)
- **Argument:** New Luxury is usually created by category outsiders (or insiders who think like outsiders), emotionally attached to the product, who refuse the conventional price-volume curve. Goods are accessible but not ubiquitous; often “mass-artisanal.” Retail polarization and capital-market access enable scale; eight practices recur.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Outsider thinking; optimal unit volume (not too scarce, not too common); mass-artisanal; specialty retail + discounters as New Luxury channels; innovation cascade/collapse; eight practices restated with examples; warning against cosmetic “luxury” badges (e.g., Cimarron).
- **Companies/episodes:** Ely Callaway; Pleasant Rowland/American Girl; Fred Carl Jr./Viking; Jim Koch/Samuel Adams; Joe Coulombe/Trader Joe’s (from Pronto); Wexner/Victoria’s Secret; Ronald Shaich/Panera (via Saint Louis Bread); Joe Foster/Whirlpool Duet; Edward Phillips/Belvedere & Chopin; Zara/Inditex/Ortega; Wal-Mart “fast fashion” & Cathy Horyn sandals anecdote; Absolut brand-image contrast vs Belvedere/Grey Goose; Cadillac Cimarron caution.
- **Numbers committed:** BMW U.S. ~213k vehicles (2001) vs GM ~4.2M; Mondavi Woodbridge >6,600 cases ($6–$10) vs Coastal 1,290 ($10–$15). Olivier olives ~50% premium for hand-placement. Zara: up to ~10k new items/year; manufacturing costs ~20% higher; Inditex profits +31% (2001). Wal-Mart apparel/accessories ~$25B/year. Williams-Sonoma revenue/profit growth cited for 2002. Panera ~$6 sandwich example; Nutro nutrient story. IPO wave examples: Tiffany 1987, Callaway Golf 1992, Starbucks 1992, Boston Beer 1995, Martha Stewart Living 1999, Coach (listed among creators). Whirlpool shattered ~$1,000 appliance ceiling assumption.
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 65–86

### Chapter Four — Inside the New American Home (approx PDF page 87–112)
- **Argument:** Home is the #1 trading-up category. Larger, more open houses + equity fuel spend; specialty home retailers and appliance innovators (esp. Duet) convert technical gains into emotional attachment; Old retail stuck in the middle loses.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Home as identity; open floor plan / master suite; Good–Better–Best–Premium merchandising (Lowe’s); style arbiters; eight practices applied to Williams-Sonoma/Pottery Barn/Crate & Barrel/Whirlpool.
- **Companies/episodes:** Lowe’s vs Home Depot; Chuck Williams & Howard Lester/Williams-Sonoma; Pottery Barn transformation; Martha Stewart impressions; Maytag Neptune vs Whirlpool Calypso vs Duet; Gordon Segal/Crate & Barrel direct sourcing; West Elm extension.
- **Numbers committed:** Avg home size 1,000 sq ft (1950) → 1,500 (1970) → >2,200 (2000); ownership 55% → 63% → >68% (2001). New homes 2.5+ baths 16%→56%; two-car garages 39%→83%; central AC 34%→85% (1970–2000). Lot/house ratio 10:1 (1985) → 5.8:1 (2000). 1950s kitchen ~$9k vs today’s >$57k (real 2002$). Lowe’s ~$2.8B → ~$20B in 1990s; ~$35B market value created; sales +18%/yr 1997–2001; comps +6%, total sales +20%, earnings +43% in 2001–02 downturn. Williams-Sonoma >$2.1B (2001) from $287M (1990); ~$2.4B market value; >$600/sq ft; Pottery Barn to ~$1B brand. Martha Stewart ~88M impressions/month (2001 peak claim). Industry experts once capped front-load share at 5–6%; Duet launched 2001 after Calypso 1998.
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 87–112

- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Fast casual economics; story/creative building blocks; value-chain customization; apostles/word-of-mouth; insider-as-outsider (Shaich).
- **Companies/episodes:** Panera/Ronald Shaich (Saint Louis Bread → sell Au Bon Pain); Chipotle & Pret A Manger (McDonald’s ventures via Lederhausen); Cheesecake Factory/David Overton & Grand Lux Café; Trader Joe’s/Joe Coulombe (private-label adventure retail).

Trader Joe’s ~85% private label; ~10k sq ft stores; ~$2B franchise via word of mouth (authors).

### Chapter Six — Awakening the American Palate to Wine (approx PDF page 135–157)
- **Argument:** U.S. wine traded up in two waves: Mondavi raised fine-wine ambition; Jess Jackson democratized “really good wines average people could afford” via blending science, awards, and retail muscle—redrawing the demand curve.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Emotional spaces in wine; taste-profile-first blending (“Flavor Domaine,” True Coast); residual fructose/tartness for middle palate; awards + large retail sales force vs three-tier friction; critic scoring demystifies category; New World competitive pressure (Australia).
- **Companies/episodes:** Robert Mondavi (fumé blanc; winery as tourist magnet); Jess Jackson & Jed Steele/Kendall-Jackson; Robert Parker / Wine Spectator; Australian export boom.
- **Numbers committed:** KJ Vintner’s Reserve raised above $10 in early 1990s quality defense; portfolio from accessible premium to Stature $70+. Australia vine acreage doubled 1988–98; world export volume share 1%→5%; export sales +45%/yr; price/liter +8%/yr; Australia ~3% of world wine production. Wine Spectator/Advocate circulation rose sharply 1985–2000 (qualitative). Cost note: producing a $14 bottle ≈3% of retail price in Australia vs ~7.5% similar U.S. wine (as stated).
- **Anchor:** PDF pages 135–157

### Chapter Seven — The World Is a Sexy Place: Victoria’s Secret (approx PDF page 158–171)
- **Argument:** Wexner’s specialty-retail thesis applied to lingerie: own the emotional space of sexy everyday fashion underwear via branded stores, bra credibility, and continuous reinvention—breaking the everyday vs special-occasion compromise.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Specialist not generalist; ladder alignment (fit/quality + fashion + sexy emotion); owned retail as experience/channel advantage; supermodel apostles; perpetual outsider attack via newness.
- **Companies/episodes:** Wexner biography (Limited 1963); purchase of Victoria’s Secret (~1982); English Lace & Dream Angels bra launches (1997) and subsequent fit/quality push; catalog-to-store transformation.

### Chapter Eight — The Old World in New Luxury Bottles: Belvedere, Boston Beer (approx PDF page 172–190)
- **Argument:** Premium spirits and craft beer show New Luxury can invade “old” liquid categories: technical authenticity + storytelling + influencer seeding (bartenders/celebrities; bar-by-bar selling) against consolidated mass incumbents.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Badge/called-drink dynamics; Questing palate education; Connecting; Phillips platform (4× distill, charcoal, heritage, creamy finish); bartender co-creation; Koch contract brewing + ingredient control + freshness dating; selective distribution; later line extension (Sam Adams Light).
- **Companies/episodes:** Edward Phillips/Millennium Import/Belvedere; Grey Goose/Ketel One/Absolut comparisons; Jim Koch & Rhonda Kallman/Boston Beer vs Anheuser-Busch/Miller/Coors consolidation.
- **Numbers committed:** Premium spirits volume +40% (1995–2001) while nonpremium −5%; bourbon CAGR 2.7%, scotch blends 5.2% (context table). Belvedere on-premise ~55–60% of volume; ~$15 Belvedere martini trade-up. Beer: ~2,000 independents → three nationals ~80% share; A-B ~$13B, 21% operating profit, ~$1B capex/yr; ~49% volume / ~72% profit; 10M-bbl brewery cost ~20% below 500k-bbl. Boston Beer sales growth ~40%/yr 1984–1996; ~$250M sales (as “largest specialty brewer”); Sam Adams Light up to ~25% of beer sales (2002 intro); priced ~50% above segment leader; little advertising first decade.

### Chapter Nine — Demonstrably Superior and Pleasingly Different: Callaway (approx PDF page 191–207)
- **Argument:** Ely Callaway’s slogan and career model New Luxury entrepreneurship: technical leap (oversized driver) that makes weekend golf more fun, seeded via influencers, then relentless innovation—without promising lower scores.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Popularize elite game; “demonstrably superior and pleasingly different”; influence/event marketing over mass ads; apostle owners; outsider attack of stagnant category.
- **Companies/episodes:** Callaway’s textile career → winery → Callaway Golf; Big Bertha intro 1991; titanium/R&D path; Johnny Miller spokesman; celebrity endorsers (Leonard, Smokey Robinson, Gates); Jake golfer reprise.
- **Numbers committed:** WWII procurement scale ~$700M/yr under his jurisdiction; Big Bertha era: net sales +≥$100M each year 1991–98; corporate sales more than doubled to $54.7M early on; IPO 1992; peak market value $2.5B on $849M sales / $214M operating income. Chi Chi Rodriguez anecdote: $250k offer refused. Jake ~$18/hr, <$50k, three-index.

### Chapter Eleven — A Cautionary Tale of an Old Luxury Brand: Cadillac (approx PDF page 221–236)
- **Argument:** Cadillac once owned American Old Luxury emotion but lost when technical/functional benefits lagged rivals; emotion alone cannot hold durables long. BMW/Mercedes/Lexus show New Luxury ladders and range extension; Cadillac’s CTS revival may still lack a clear emotional space win.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Benefit-ladder failure → dethroning (durables < decade; consumables ≤ ~2 years); Old Luxury status vs New Luxury engagement; brand-range architecture (BMW 7/5/3 + MINI separate).
- **Companies/episodes:** Cadillac history (Leland 1902–03; Durant/GM); postwar chrome icon; Cimarron earlier caution echoed; Lexus residual-value contrast; BMW psychographics & MINI launch; Mercedes mix shift; Cadillac CTS/Northstar/Bondurant alliances.
- **Numbers committed:** Model A $750/$850 (~25–50% premiums). Durant bought Cadillac for $5.5M. Residual: Mercedes up to ~70% after 3 years vs Cadillac −35% immediately (as stated). BMW profit $1.87B (cited as highest among majors in period); 3-Series from $27,800; 5-Series from $37,600; 67% of 2001 BMW sales from those two; MINI Cooper S $19,425 (~29% below base 3-Series); BMW <2% U.S. share. Mercedes 1980 mix 70% mid / 21% entry / 9% high → later 45% mid / 28% low / 21% high / 6% other. CTS sales +16% (2002). Dealer investment notes $5–10M.

### Chapter Thirteen — A Call to Action (approx PDF page 268–271)
