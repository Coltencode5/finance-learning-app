### Pre-Existing Conditions (approx PDF page 31–51)
- **Argument:** COVID killed retailers with pre-existing weakness (debt, dying channels, irrelevance); first wave predictable; a second wave threatens otherwise healthy firms via a “fractional future” of capped occupancy, fear, and spending restraint cascading into white-collar cuts and CRE. Generations differently sidelined. Mortality-salience psychology (Becker → Solomon et al.) predicts crisis shopping: safety/distraction → value/belonging → legacy/afterlife. Habit formation may stick after ~600 days of new behaviors; retail follows life, not vice versa.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Pre-existing conditions analogy; fractional future; mortality salience / world-view reconstruction (safety & distraction; value & belonging / lipstick effect; legacy & afterlife / secular immortality); habit myth (21 days) vs ~66 days; ~600-day pandemic habit window.
- **Companies/episodes:** Coresight US closures; UK bankruptcies; Aldo (Canada); German bankruptcy moratoria; long NA bankruptcy list (JCPenney, Neiman Marcus, J.Crew, GNC, Lord & Taylor, etc.); Theory’s Andrew Rosen; Westlake Center empty; 9/11 as real-world mortality-salience test.

### The Wormhole (approx PDF page 52–86)
- **Argument:** Modern retail is an industrial-era artifact (factory schools → cities → department stores → suburbs/malls → tech re-urbanization). COVID + digital portability enable a historic reversal: WFH, suburbanization 2.0, education disruption, transit/air-travel collapse, and digital substitutes for experiences. Not acceleration — a wormhole into a post-digital era with permanently reshaped life and shopping.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Industrial foundations of retail; WFH as structural (not temporary); cities de-densify but don’t die; education as retail-like disruption ripe for tech hybrids; experiential trade-off (good-enough tech + better model beats fidelity); higher bar for IRL time/money spent.

### Resurrecting Retail (approx PDF page 239–249)
- **Argument:** Closing call via Selfridge’s 1918 *Romance of Commerce*: industry gained clever devices but lost theater and progressive ethics. COVID is a chance to rebuild. Brands fill trust vacuum left by church/state. Redesign supply chains for uncertainty/resilience (not lowest cost); stop “less bad,” pursue circular good (McDonough); circularity includes wages/equity, not only materials. Future leaders practice **H.E.R.O.** (Humility, Empathy, Resilience, Openness). From ashes: purposeful archetypal retailers + community-center malls.
- **Mechanisms/frameworks:** Brands as new church and state; build for uncertainty not cost (Thorbeck / Chainge; Zara speed contrast); cradle-to-cradle / stop being less bad; circular business (Ellen MacArthur + social inequality); H.E.R.O. leadership.
- **Companies/episodes:** H. Gordon Selfridge (1918 book); Chainge Capital / John Thorbeck; Zara; William McDonough; women-led pandemic responses (Ardern, Merkel, Marin, Tsai) as leadership analogs.
- **Numbers committed:** Pew 2019: only 17% Americans trust government “do what is right” (vs 77% in 1964); UK poll >33k: >⅔ feel unrepresented; 72% US consumers say gov/political leaders divide society; 75% Swedes 16–29 no religious affiliation; Edelman 2018 (8k consumers / 8 markets): ~⅔ buy on brand social/political stance; 53% believe brands can do more than governments on social problems; Bangladesh garment legal min wage 8,000 taka (~$95/month); US retail fight for $15/hr (~$31k/yr).
